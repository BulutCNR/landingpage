The problem

Most small businesses have never had a security audit. Not because they are secure — because they assume they are not targets. That assumption is the vulnerability.

Automated tools match patterns against known signatures. They cannot evaluate whether your authentication flow is logically broken, whether error messages leak stack traces, or whether an attacker can reach admin functions through a regular user account.

A real audit requires someone who understands the application in context. Not a scanner running templates. A person who can reason about what your specific setup actually exposes.

CNRCO combines automated scanning with manual testing, maps findings to real compliance requirements, and provides a named auditor who stands behind the work.

What gets tested

Three-phase assessment process

Each engagement follows a structured methodology: reconnaissance, automated and manual vulnerability testing, risk scoring, and compliance mapping. Scope is agreed in writing before work begins.

PHASE 01 — Reconnaissance

Information Gathering

  • WHOIS and DNS enumeration
  • SSL/TLS certificate inspection
  • HTTP headers and stack fingerprinting
  • Subdomain enumeration
  • SPF, DKIM, DMARC configuration
  • Open port and service discovery

PHASE 02 — Assessment

Vulnerability Testing

  • OWASP Top 10 — automated and manual
  • SQL injection — manual payloads
  • XSS — stored, reflected, DOM-based
  • Broken access control and IDOR
  • Authentication and session review
  • API endpoint discovery and testing
  • Dependency CVE scanning
  • Secrets in code and .git history

PHASE 03 — Risk scoring

Prioritization and Mapping

  • CVSS v3.1 — base, temporal, environmental
  • Business impact ranking
  • OWASP Top 10 compliance status
  • GDPR readiness assessment
  • PCI-DSS applicability review
  • ISO 27001 gap analysis

Accountability gap

Why automated tools are not enough

Scanners and AI-generated reports handle initial triage. They are not a substitute for professional accountability — and regulators know the difference.

Accountability

No one to hold responsible

If an automated tool misses a critical vulnerability, there is no professional liability. CNRCO audits are signed by a named auditor. If something is in scope and was missed, that accountability exists.

Context

Pattern matching vs. real assessment

Scanners match signatures. They do not understand whether your password reset flow is logically broken, whether access controls are enforced server-side, or whether your authentication design makes sense for your risk profile.

Threat awareness

Historical data vs. current attack patterns

AI is trained on historical data. A human auditor understands current exploitation techniques, active CVEs being used in the wild, and how attackers chain vulnerabilities — not just what was documented at training cutoff.

Compliance proof

AI output is not auditable

GDPR Article 32 requires demonstrating appropriate technical measures. A regulatory body or insurer will not accept an AI-generated output as evidence of a professional security review. A signed audit report is.

Remediation

A list vs. a prioritized plan

Automated tools produce lists. A CNRCO audit produces a prioritized remediation roadmap with effort estimates, code-level guidance, and timelines — so your team knows what to fix first and how.

Business risk

CVSS score vs. your actual exposure

A CVSS 7.5 vulnerability may be low-risk in your environment, or critical given your specific data handling. Business impact ranking requires understanding your operations, not just the technical severity metric.
